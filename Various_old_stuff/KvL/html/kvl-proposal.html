Proposal for the Kwaliteit van Leven project

period 15-07-08 to 31-12-08

The major aim for the Quality of Life project has been to investigate how to utilize current genomic data for breeding practices. In the course of this project a tool has been developed called FanSNiP (http://scomp1038.wur.nl/FANsnp/kvl_toolbox.cgi). This tool has been conceived to facilitate a 'high troughput implementation of the candidate gene approach', automating the whole process from finding candidate genes to finding putative functional mutations in those genes, not limiting the researcher to just a few genes but making it possible to even use the whole of the transcriptome as the starting point of a candidate gene approach, and still needing only hours, or days at most, to get a comprehensive list of candidate genes and SNPs. The tool was never meant to be a black box, but rather a protocol to demonstrate how the content of various databases could be smartly searched and combined, hammering home the message that one need not limit oneself to just a few favorite genes.

For the next stage of the KvL project it was proposed to return to the initial research aims and cases. We set out to see how current knowledge on genomics could increase our understanding of trade-off phenotypes in animal breeding. The cases that were provided all revolved around decreased fertility or mothering capabilities in animals that have been very heavily selected for meat (or milk) production. Such trade-off traits are very difficult to deal with using tradition breeding practices, and it is believed that genomic information may add to solving problems involving such traits.

Text-based searches using OMIM and Pubmed are a logical starting point to directly investigate which genes could be related to any the phenotypes. Gene Set Enrichment analysis, which is now a filtering option in Fansnip, will provide additional insight into the pathways involved as can be derived from the GO annotations of the genes. These GO annotations themselves could be further used to investigate a) where pathways intersect and b) to select additional genes that could be involved in the phenotype while not explicitly searchable through pubmed and OMIM. In addition, the pathways will be further elucidated using the gene-interaction options provided by FANSNIP. Ultimately, a list of genes could be further narrowed down by only selecting genes that are expressed in relevant tissues (e.g. the gonads) or life stage. If available QTL information can also be used to further narrow down a list of candidate genes.

For each step in the search process it will be made explicit what databases were searched and how, this way providing more insight into the 'protocol' that essentially constitutes the tool. Along the way, it is likely that adjustments will have to be made to the Fansnip tool to accommodate current inefficiencies or inadequacies..

The ultimate outcome should be:

1)Increased insight in genes involved in trade-off characters

2)These results should be compared to the report of prof. Decuypere

3)Description of the Fansnip tool as a protocol for finding genes and SNPs

4)preparation of a paper describing these outcomes

5)increased functionality and utilization of the Fansnip tool

A scientific paper, even if not aimed at a high-ranking journal, should be one of our aims. While it may be difficult to present this as a 'novel' tool, particularly as it will be difficult to open the fansnip website to a general public, the outcome from a case study may provide enough interesting insights both into the biology and utilization of resources.


A sample query


A text based query was done both for fertility (female sided) and growth related terms. Pubmed and OMIM were searched with the terms 'female and fertility'. A 'growth' related search was set up by applying 'body size and growth' to OMIM and “gene and growth and muscle and size” to Pubmed. Combining the 'fertility' searches yielded 557 genes involved in this 'trait', while 'growth' yielded 478 genes.

To filter out spurious hits as much as possible and to end up with genes that fall into groups of pathways, a Gene Set Enrichment analysis was done to select only genes with significant associations (p<0.00001). A total of 351 and 335 genes passed this filtering step, the results are shown in 1) and 2).

The next step was to combine both searches, and to see which genes were in common. In total, 94 genes were returned which can be viewed in (3) (including associated GO terms). Given that the constraint (fertility) is likely related to the tissue 'ovary' it was decided to only take into account those genes that are expressed in this tissue. It turned out that 50 of 94 genes were expressed at a level of >10 TPM (Transcripts Per Million) (4).

By using the default options in Fansnip and combining multiple runs a list of 50 candidate genes that may be important in both fertility and growth related pathways were thus identified. With this straightforward approach, many of the 'suspects' turn up. The one everybody would expect, as this has been the focal point of several of our previous discussions on the subject, is IGF1. IGF2 is not there as it is not expressed in the chicken ovary at >10 TPM but several other genes in the 'insulin receptor signaling pathway' (GO:0008286) such as IGF1 and IGF2 receptors and an IGF binding protein are there, resulting in a significant (p<0.0001) test score in the GSE test (5).

Next to the IGF-related genes, several other well-known growth factors and receptors are identified that were also suggested by prof. Decuypere (Table 7 in his report), such as BMP, GH and TNF, EGF, FGF. One of few not identified here and perhaps the most conspicuous is LEP, which did not turn up in the text based searches (although LEPR did but did not pass GSE filtering). GDF8 is perhaps one of the more interesting ones not identified by prof. Decuypere.

Perhaps equally importantly a number of lesser known genes were selected. Many of these genes turn out to be transcription factors, which seems to be consistent with most of the GO terms being of the 'regulation of development' and 'regulation of transcription' type. If one compares this to the GO associations of 'fertility' (1) and 'growth' (2), this suggests that the overlap between reproduction and growth is 'higher up' in the regulation pathways. I have not quantified this really, although combining the GO terms associated with both text based searches do suggest that specific 'reproduction' or 'growth' related GO terms tend to be non-overlapping, while regulation related GO-terms are (data not shown).

Even this relatively straightforward query shows that many of the ideas on important 'trade-off genes' can be reproduced using the protocols implemented in Fansnip. Perhaps of even more interest additional growth factors could be identified, as well as a considerable number of other genes, many of which are transcription factors. These genes, in retrospect, make lots of sense but would perhaps not have been identified so readily using 'traditional' methods. In fact, Fansnip gives even more clues as it also provides – through the GO analysis – hints on pathways, pathways which can be further analysed using other methods implemented in Fansnip. This will be – a.o. - the focus for the remaining time in the project.

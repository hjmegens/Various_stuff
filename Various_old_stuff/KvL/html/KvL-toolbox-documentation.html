Author: H.-J. Megens

Introduction


Over the past years a wealth of genomic information has become available for the species that are the main focus for animal breeding in the Netherlands. The genomes of cattle and chicken are in an advanced stage of being sequenced and annotated, and for both species a large variation database is also available. While for pig this stage has currently (February 2008) not yet been reached, rapid progress is expected, with the first draft chromosome sequences currently being deposited in EnsEMBL. The major breeding companies in the Netherlands, i.e. Institute for Pig Genetics (IPG), Holland Genetics and Hendrix Genetics, in collaboration with Wageningen UR – Animal Breeding and Genomics Center and with financial support from Center, have set up a collaborative effort aimed at better utilizing the information that is now publicly available.

The project not only aims at utilizing genomic information of cattle, pig and chicken, but also aims at implementation of knowledge obtained from model species such as mouse, rat and human. Such information becomes particularly relevant for features that are relatively well annotated in a species such as human. One can think about annotations of regulatory elements, but also GO terms and Kegg pathways. In addition, for model species expression and gene-gene interaction data is becoming available that could be used to elucidate gene pathways or gene networks involved in interesting phenotypes for commercial breeding.

Accessing all this information provides several major challenges. First of all, much of the information currently available is highly fragmented. This means that one has to query multiple databases to acquire the information needed. Moreover, access to certain databases is not straightforward and getting the right information in the right format is often a daunting task.

Combining all the data is labor-intensive. Just for a single candidate gene one could spend many hours getting all the information, from literature search to getting SNPs. For larger SNP arrays to type variation in a whole pathway, a manual search of SNPs becomes even almost impractical. For medical studies tools are becoming available to aid in selecting SNPs for association studies. However, for animal breeding such tools are not available.


The toolbox: rationale


Let us hypothesize that one of the breeding companies is interested in a particular phenotype and its genetic basis. Current practices can largely be divided in two strategies: QTL analysis or a candidate gene approach. Both approaches have their strengths and pitfalls. QTL analysis can potentially generate information regarding genomic regions involved in phenotypes using anonymous markers and no prior knowledge on genetic pathways involved in the phenotype of interest. However, one requires a fairly high density of markers and very expensive experimental breeding designs.

The promise of a candidate gene approach, on the other hand, is to target only a small number of loci (even perhaps a single SNP) with a possible association to a phenotype. The candidate gene approach, however, is labor intensive in the sense that prior knowledge, e.g. from model organisms, is required and SNPs furthermore are chosen for high likelihood of representing functional mutations, which requires additional analyses. Another disadvantage of the candidate gene approach is that it is either 'hit or mis' – chances of any one gene, and the SNPs therein, would be involved in the phenotype to a sufficiently large effect to be detectable in a moderate to small experimental breeding design are not very large, although they may be enlarged by adding knowledge regarding functional aspects of the variation under study.

Candidate gene studies have traditionally involved small numbers of genes and variations. Cost of genotyping has been a major factor in keeping such studies low-throughput. However, with costs of custom genotyping assays dropping rapidly in the past years, studies will become less limited by the overall size of the genotyping effort. Therefore, the limitations for 'high throughput candidate gene approach' are shifting more towards the time and effort involved in finding candidate genes, and finding variation within or near those genes that is likely to affect the function of these genes.

A 'high throughput candidate gene approach' would enlarge the chance of finding functional variation, while still being in a genotyping format of say 400 to 1500 snps (upper end of Illumina VeraCode and Sentrix Arrays, respectively) that are too small for a genome-wide high-density QTL analysis. The added advantage of candidate gene studies is that it can address the genetics of problems in breeding that are very difficult to solve with conventional breeding practices. One can particularly think about trade-off problems; when lines are very strongly selected for one trait, e.g. growth or meat production, this often creates problems in other traits of the animals that are less strongly selected, such as fertility.

With more and more information becoming available regarding functions of genes, expression, regulation, interactions and variation, candidate gene approaches could potentially become more and more powerful. At the same time, however, the number of genes known to be potentially involved in a phenotype is also growing. A high throughput candidate gene study, therefore, becomes impractical to design by hand, and there is a need for automation.

The 'Kwaliteit van Leven' project aims at the development of research tools for better understanding the relationship between phenotype and genotype. It specifically aims at utilizing much of the genomics resources that are becoming available. The tools are 'modules', little programs designed to perform a specific task. These tasks vary from retrieving, combining and manipulating information from databases to analysis on data implementing third party software and combining and manipulating results from such analysis.


Manual


Setting up the workspace


You need an account on the lx3 server to use the Toolbox. At this stage you can only access it while working at the Zodiac building. You can access the lx3 by using ssh (secure shell) login. If you are using a windows workstation, use an ssh client such as Putty. It is also a good idea to install an SFTP client tool such as WinSCP3 to move files to and from the lx3.


Log into the lx3. Type: KvL.


Select your species of choice by typing the number and <ENTER>. Please note that, because the pig genome is not yet available, functionality for this species is very limited (currently – almost - none). Note that as soon as the program is invoked, a folder will be created in your working directory called kvltempXXXXXXXX (X can be any hexadecimal). The unique, randomly generated name will ensure that no two result folders are the same which is important for certain automated processes. Some have complained about the awkward names. There is another option where you can generate folder names of your own choice:


% KvL -t mydir


where 'mydir' can be replaced by any name you want (avoid spaces in dirnames!). As it is possible that you inadvertently choose a name twice, choose illegal characters or otherwise compromise the directory assignment procedure, it is recommended to use this option with caution.


Gene Search


After selecting a species, you'll be prompted with a menu of options about how you want to input your genes. You can input genes directly, either by typing a gene name directly at the command line (option 1), or giving a list of gene names from a text file (option 6). If you want you can also give an EnsEMBL identifier (option 7), but this has been mainly put in for development purposes.

There are alternative ways to put genes into the pipeline. These gene-selection tools work by retrieving genes from a region on the genome (e.g. QTL region), option 2, by searching the GO database for associated genes (option 3), by doing a PubMed search (option 4), by searching OMIM (option 5), by retrieving all the genes that have interactions with a particular gene, (option 8), by inputting two genes and retrieving all the genes that are in common in the networks (option 9), or by expression level (option 10).

The menu is designed to be self-explanatory, but here is some additional information and pointers.


Option 1: input a single gene name


Genes can now be retrieved by species-specific gene names. In the past, only official human gene names (HGNC-database) could be used. The rationale behind this being that this is currently the best curated database of names available. This creates minimum of ambiguous results, and getting to the orthologue in you favorite species would be done through the EnsEMBL Compara database. However, as many of you pointed out, this was not a desirable situation as it would often lead to unsatisfactory results because the gene would often not be found in the Hugo database. As an alternative, searches currently start taking the NCBI Gene database as a reference. This is a database containing lots of species-specific gene names, and by implementing it, chances of finding genes has increased. However, it has also demonstrated new problems. 1) This database is not maintained to the degree of the Hugo database, and can therefore never be used as a reference database 2) Because I will never allow ambiguous results (e.g. Multiple hits) to occur – which would undermine the whole idea of automated searches – alias names are not taken into account so if you do not have the 'correct' name you'll still won't find it 3) As the pipeline is designed to take much of the necesary information from EnsEMBL, everything depends on proper cross database annotations. Currently, many genes are not properly cross-databased for many non-model species including cattle and chicken. This means that although you will find an entry in NCBI based on your search string, you will still not get results. I tried to overcome this by then falling back to the old approach of going through Hugo, but this is not ideal for several reasons.

Example: search for bovine DGAT1. The pipeline will find this name in the local implementation of the NCBI Gene database, and will retrieve an NCBI gene ID (282609). Then it gets stuck, however, because there is no proper reference to EnsEMBL. Currently the pipeline tries to overcome this by falling back to the old system of finding names in the Hugo database. Again, it finds this name and proper gene Ids (both for NCBI and EnsEMBL), but then gets stuck because there is no proper one-to-one ortholog of the gene in cattle. Bottom line: DGAT1 does not return any results because a) cross database annotation is imperfect and b) cross species annotation is imperfect.


Option 2: retrieve genes from a genomic region


This is a search option that will directly take the annotated genes from EnsEMBL for a particular region. As many genes are 'predicted' or otherwise poorly annotated, it will very often result in returning genes with unclear function. Note that currently no check is build in with regard to 'out of bound' searches, which means that there is very likely that the pipeline will simply crash if you search on a non-existing piece of genomic real-estate.


Option 3: retrieve genes by querying the GO database


Currently, this will retrieve human genes, and these are subsequently orthologised for our favorite species. GO (Gene Ontology) searches can be done from a GO term or GO ID as a starting point. The pipeline is designed to give hints in case a particular GO term is not found, but this is currently implemented in a minimalistic way. Parent and child terms of the GO term will be displayed, and all genes annotated to the GO term AND its child terms will be retrieved.


Option 4: find genes based on a PubMed search


Will find genes based on a text search in the PubMed archives by retrieving gene ids that are annotated to the PubMed ids. Currently designed to retrieve human, mouse and rat genes, AND the genes of your favorite species. However, a pubmed search on 'cattle' and 'milk' may not result in a SNP search for 'DGAT', for instance, although the gene is found through the PubMed search. The same limitations as those in Option 1 are set here. Please note that during working hours Eastern Time (USA), the limit is set to retrieving information from (the last) 200 pubmed hits. After working hours, that number is set at 5000. There is also a built in delay between retrieving hits, all of this to prevent the NCBI site from being overburdened (NCBI makes it a habit just to refuse IP addresses that misbehave; as the whole of WUR is using the same IP address, it is possible for a single person in WUR to shut down the service for all). It is recommended to narrow your search string or plan your search during . Alternatively, contact HJM for temporary disabling limitations.


Option 5: find genes based on an OMIM search


Will find human genes which are orthologised to genes in your favorite species.


Option 6: input multiple genes from a text file


Same as option 1, but designed to input large (basically unlimited) numbers of genes. Same limitations apply as to Option 1. What is needed is a list of gene names, one gene name per line, in a text file. The text file should be in your working directory. If it is not, specify the path to the file.


Option 7: input a gene by EnsEMBL identifier


Mainly usefull for development purposes.


Option 8: retrieve genes in the network neighborhood of your favorite gene


This option will receive human genes only, as for human gene-gene interactions are best defined of any vertebrate species. The gene-gene interactions are as databased in NCBI, which in turn derives this information from other sources (BIND, etc). You will be prompted for setting the depth of a network. Minimum is 1, the gene of choice + all its direct interactants. Maximum is 4, at which depth you will probably retrieve (provided there is any interaction data at all for your gene of choice) a substantial part of the gene universe. The


Option 9: get genes that are common in the networks of two genes


Similar approach as option 8. However, you will be prompted for two genes, and for a depth of the network. What will be returned is the part of the network these two genes have in common. This could, for instance, be usefull to see which genes could be involved in trade-off phenotypes.


Option 10: find genes expressed in a particular tissue


Upon selecting this option, you will be presented with a list of tissues. The tissues are species specific, and the definition of the tissue is according to the UniGene database. You can select one or more tissues, in case you have more than one tissue separate tissue numbers with a space, and press enter. You will then be prompted for an expression level (10, 100 or 1000 transcripts per million -TPM), and (in case you have selected multiple tissues) for a way how to combine tissues (AND, OR, NOT). The expression data is derived from EST-library sequencing studies, not from array data. The data can be explored manually by going to NCBI-UniGene-Expression profiles.

I have nevertheless opted to post the new version on the lx3, because Marko's project mandated he used the expression data.


By invoking one of these options, one or more genes are retrieved and searched automatically for a number of features. Apart from output to the screen (unless the 'silent' option is invoked), you will also be presented with a number of reports. These reports are filed in the directory 'results' created created in the master directory that was created at the start of the search. (simultaneously two other subdirectories were created: errorlog and temp. These are only important for the program itself, and for debugging purposes).

The results of the 'gensearch' can be found in the gensearch.txt and gensearch.htm files. These files contain identical information, in the .txt file presented as flat text, in the .htm in hyper text markup format which can be displayed by your web browser. (NOTE: I have apparently managed to program exotic tags in my CGI scripts that MS Explorer can't display. Firefox works fine. Working on that).


SNP search


There are three more files in the 'results' directory that hold information on the second part of the search: the search for SNP variation that is potentially of relevance with respect to phenotypic variation. The SNPreport.txt file is a flat text file containing only this information, and basically summarizes relevant information. The other two files, fullreport.txt and fullreport.html are identical in content but again different in markup (.txt is flat text file, .html can be viewed by your web browser). These 'fullreport' files shows (potentially) interesting information apart from the SNP variations.


  1. The list of genes (EnsEMBL Ids) and how these genes were obtained

  2. Next, information is listed for each of the genes:

    1. EnsEMBL ID and species

    2. homology type (redundant as currently this is done in GenSearch). Currently, the pipeline takes by default both only one-to-one relationships into account. A one-to-many relationship will return multiple 'orthologs', which the pipeline can not handle because this means there may multiple and ambiguous answers to a procedure that expects a single straight solution. I am currently working on an algorithm that could mitigate this problem.

    3. Description of the gene (description as databased in EnsEMBL

    4. External references (some possibly relevant external refs as databased in EnsEMBL).

    5. Position of the gene in the selected species.

    6. UniGene expression profile, as well as expression information from the Human Protein Reference Database (done by using human orthologues).

    7. Any regulatory elements that are annotated in EnsEMBL (currently only relevant for human genome, but in the future more and more for other genomes).

    8. The program FootPrinter is implemented in the pipeline to look for conserved motifs in the regions upstream of transcription start. FootPrinter does not look at positional homologies, but rather looks for small (10 to 6 bp) motifs that are occurring in multiple sequences. These motifs might represent transcription factor binding sites (TFBS). To increase the likelihood of finding relevant motifs, A 500bp upstream region of your favorite animal is compared to orthologous upstream regions of several other species (human, mouse, chicken, cattle, dog and rat). These orthologous regions are derived from EnsEMBL database, and a input file for FootPrinter is generated. FootPrinter output is redirected to the pipeline, and relevant sites are displayed. The pipeline automatically scans predicted TFBS for SNP information based on variation in dbSNP. (NOTE: the FootPrinter option is currently broken, reason why is still a bit of a mystery).

    9. A second method for TFBS prediction is the implementation of the program Clover. Clover searches a single sequence (a region upstream of transcription start handed by the pipeline based on EnsEMBL annotation) for overrepresented sequences and compares this to a library of known TFBS motifs. Again, the pipeline will return relevant sites, and if present will provide SNP variation present in the motif region.

    10. Finally all transcripts will be returned for the gene, which is necesary to perform a number of transcript-specific analyses. For each of the transcripts the following will be carried out:

      1. retrieval of 3'UTR information (and sequence).

      2. retrieval of predicted miRNA binding sites from miRBase. In case a polymorphism is present in the binding site, based on polymorphism data in dbSNP, this will be returned as well.

      3. If a 3'UTR is annotated in EnsEMBL, the pipeline will do a de novo miRanda search for miRNA binding sites, using miRNA library entries specific for your favorite species. It will return variation information if applicable. One issue remaining is that positional information is sometimes not calculated accurately if the 3'UTR is coded by multiple exons. Prediction of miRNA binding sites from this analysis are probably very unreliable, and should therefore be treated as such.

Further command line options and tricks:


-s options: 'verbose' (standard) or 'silent'. The silent option redirects the standard output to a separate file. This is necessary for job processing.

-m options: 'all' (default), 'searchgenes' or 'getsnps'. Allows searching only for genes or snps. In the latter case you should have already prepared a text file containing parameters and ensembl gene ids.

-v shows the version of the toolbox


It is possible to post a job search. You should prepare a text file, e.g. Called parameter.txt, which contains the parameters as you would type them on the command line (including blank returns!).


The way to invoke the toolbox would then be:


% KvL -s silent parameter.txt


You could for instance send this command to a job processing tool such as at.


There are additional command line options, such as for unlocking the PubMed limits, which are best not shared at this time. For specific needs, contact HJM. And remember, the major goal of the toolbox was to provide... tools. Specific research questions may need a specific set of tools, and the Toolbox is here in part to demonstrate what is currently feasible. Based on the modules that are currently available it is fairly easy to make a custom analysis pipeline.
